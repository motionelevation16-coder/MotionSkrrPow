**Tweet 2 — The discovery**

I started tracking which hooks performed and which flopped.

The difference wasn't luck. It was almost always the same thing:

Specificity.

"I tried this for 30 days" kills "this changed my life" every single time.

---

**Tweet 3 — What else I found**

The other thing that keeps coming up: earned curiosity gaps vs fake ones.

"You won't believe this" = fake. Nobody cares.

"I did X and the result was the opposite of what I expected" = real tension. People click.

You can learn to feel the difference. Or you can just score it.

---

**Tweet 4 — What I built**

So I built HookScore — an API that scores your hooks before you post.

Real example:

Hook A: "This morning routine changed my life" → 58/100
Hook B: "I woke up at 4am for 30 days. Here's what nobody tells you." → 87/100

That's the /compare endpoint. Two hooks in, winner + reasoning out.

---

**Tweet 5 — Example output**

The output tells you WHY, not just what:

→ Hook B wins because: specific timeframe, implied journey, curiosity gap is earned
→ Hook A loses because: generic phrasing, no specificity, overused structure

That reasoning is what actually helps you improve. Not just a number.

---

**Tweet 6 — The 6 endpoints (part 1)**

HookScore has 6 endpoints:

→ /score — single hook, 0–100 score + grade + breakdown
→ /suggest — rewrite a weak hook into something better
→ /compare — A/B test two hooks before posting

---

**Tweet 7 — The 6 endpoints (part 2)**

→ /classify — returns hook type (curiosity / fear / authority / social proof / etc.)
→ /batch — score up to 10 hooks in one call (perfect for automation)
→ /emoji-impact — does that emoji actually help your hook, or hurt it?
